Read me first

This is a floor, not a cage

Switchboard is the minimum coordination layer so 8 people + 8 AI agents can build one feature without stepping on each other. Everything you're about to click through is how it works out of the box — a tour of what's possible, not a rulebook.

On planning day, your team decides what to keep. Want it more autonomous? Remove gates. Hate a rule? Change it. Two kinds of markers tell you what's load-bearing:

MUST breaks for everyone if you skip it — only four of these exist.

DIAL your team's call — defaults provided, change freely, write your choice into your AGENTS.md.

The four MUSTs, up front: ① every person has their own Linear account + own API key (never shared — collision safety and @-routing die without it) · ② everyone has joined the team in Linear (invite accepted ≠ team joined) · ③ someone is named owner of the Backlog sweep · ④ agents write to the board only through swb.
One picture

Every laptop is a spoke; the board is the hub

You + your agent⇄ LINEAR BOARD⇄ 7 teammates + their agents

Writes go up through swb — a small command-line tool your agent runs (you never type it). It's the only door: claims, questions, finishes all cross it, all signed 🤖 Claude — via you.

Awareness comes down automatically: every time you message your agent, a short report of what changed on the board since your last look is handed to it — and you see it as the yellow block in your terminal.

switchboard: 1 board update
@you HAC-296 Preeti: "Single id is enough here…" → swb show HAC-296

No machine ever talks to another machine. If it's not on the board, it didn't happen. That's the whole architecture — a 7th or 8th person is just one more spoke.

Before July 10 · ~10 minutes

Setup — where three of the four MUSTs live

MUST Own account, own API key. Accept the Linear invite, then mint a personal key (Linear → Settings → Security & access → Personal API keys). A shared key breaks the claim-race arbiter (both "win"), @you routing (everyone is "you"), and attribution (one person did everything).

MUST Join your team, not just the workspace. Open your team in Linear and make sure you're a member. Invite accepted ≠ team joined — we learned this live: a workspace-only member half-works (tickets fine, roster/@handles broken).

Then one line:

git clone https://github.com/BIS-Safety-Software-Inc/switchboard && cd switchboard && node install.js

The installer demands the key (with the exact click-path on screen), wires everything, sets your PATH, and ends with a health check — green wall or it tells you exactly what's wrong. Then type /swb-tour in Claude Code: your own agent walks you through everything this page describes, live, on a practice ticket — and Part 2 of that tour needs a teammate, which is the real test.

The ticket pipeline begins

Anyone proposes; everything lands in Backlog

agent: swb new→ Backlog→ GATE 1: human promotes→ Todo

Your agent (or you, in Linear) can propose unlimited tickets — they all land in Backlog. There is no code path by which an agent creates work in Todo. This is what stops a runaway loop from inventing work and assigning it to itself.

DIAL How tickets get written: mostly as a team on planning day, plus ad-hoc proposals during the build. Your call how formal the ticket template is (acceptance criteria, files to touch) — recommended, not enforced by the tool.

The one job someone must own

The Backlog sweep MUST

Name a person. PM, AI captain, or any teammate — but a name, said out loud at planning. Their job: sweep Backlog on a cadence — promote what's real, kill what's junk. Agents propose; the sweeper decides.

Why this can't be skipped: if nobody owns the sweep, agent-proposed work piles up in Backlog forever and the pipeline starves — the failure isn't chaos, it's silence. (The sweep is also your quality filter: a promoted ticket means a human read it.)

DIAL Cadence: every 30 min is the default suggestion. During a build frenzy, a fixed rhythm (:00 and :30) beats "when I remember."

DIAL The sweeper can also assign at promote time — one honest counterweight to the proposer bias (step 10).

Who takes the work — and the truth about approval

Claiming a Todo ticket

Agents don't patrol the board grabbing work. A claim starts three ways, all human-initiated:

StartWhat happens
You name it"Grab the quiz-player ticket" → agent runs swb claim
You ask "what's next?"Agent reads the Todo column (swb board), proposes one, you say yes
The digest nudgesA freshly-promoted ticket shows up in the report your agent gets with your next message. The agent doesn't act on it alone — it suggests: "HAC-31 just hit Todo and touches my files — want me to claim it?" You say yes. The nudge is information arriving; the claim is still your call.
Gate 2, honestly: if you run with permissions skipped, this gate does not exist. On paper, Gate 2 is your terminal asking "Claude wants to run: swb claim…" and you approving. In practice most devs run --dangerously-skip-permissions or broad allowlists — so the claim happens without a human seeing a prompt. What remains is after-the-fact visibility: the claim instantly appears on the board (assignee + claude chip + signed comment) and in every teammate's next digest. The team sees everything; nobody was asked first.
Gate 2 is built into the kit — and it survives --dangerously-skip-permissions. The flag turns off Claude Code's permission popups, but Switchboard's gate lives in a hook, and hooks always fire. When an agent tries swb claim or swb done, the command is denied with an instruction: ask your human in the conversation; when they say yes, re-run with --approved. So the "prompt" isn't a popup — it's your agent literally asking you in chat: "want me to claim HAC-31?" You say yes. That exchange IS Gate 2, and no flag removes it.

Honest fine print: the handshake trusts the agent to actually ask before adding --approved. A misbehaving agent could lie — but the flag is logged and the ticket comment says "human-approved," so a lie is on the record forever. This is agent-grade trust, same as everything else agents do.

DIAL Teams that WANT autonomous claiming (finish → grab next, nobody asked) set "gate2": "off" in the repo's .swb.json. One line, decided Friday, out loud — that single line is the difference between human-assigned work and a self-serve swarm.

What a claim actually does

One claim = five protections

swb claim HAC-31 --files "src/player/**"
  1. Board: you become assignee, status → In Progress — everyone's digest hears it.
  2. Chip: a claude (or codex) label lands on the row — at a glance, an agent took this.
  3. Signed comment: 🤖 Claude — via Turni Saha + the declared files — the audit trail.
  4. Worktree: a separate working copy of the repo on its own branch — parallel work can't trample.
  5. File ownership: the declared globs (wildcard patterns — src/player/** = everything under that folder) are recorded. If anyone else's agent later edits a matching file, their terminal warns them with your name and ticket. Warns — never blocks.

And the agent gets the full ticket text printed into its context at claim time — it never builds from just a title. (No description on the ticket? It's told to go ask before building.)

DIAL WIP limit — one claim per person at a time is the recommended default. It is a rule, not code: nothing stops hoarding except the board making it visible. Keep it, change it, or drop it — your call to make consciously.

The yellow block, and its honest limit

How everyone stays current without asking

Every message you send your agent, a report of board changes since your last look rides along — new tickets, claims, questions for you (always sorted first, full text). You see the same thing as a solid yellow block; your agent is told to state the impact before continuing if anything touches its lane.

switchboard: 2 board updates
@you HAC-296 Preeti: "Single id is enough here. The practice schema is one entity…" → swb show HAC-296
claim HAC-295 tour: Preeti — practice → Preeti files: src/preeti/**
The honest limit — idle means blind. Delivery is guaranteed when you prompt. While your terminal sits idle, nothing arrives; a question for you waits on the board until your next message. During an active build day that's usually seconds. Real-time push is a known v2 item — deliberately cut so nothing on 16 laptops can silently die.

Want the whole picture instead of changes? swb board — every ticket by status, every assignee, on demand.

Proven live, July 7, Turni ⇄ Preeti

Ask a teammate without interrupting them

  1. Turni's agent posts: swb ask HAC-296 @Preeti "composite key or single id?" — a signed comment on the ticket. The key says where the question lives; the @name says whose digest it lands in.
  2. Preeti's next prompt → yellow block, top line @you, full question.
  3. Her agent drafts an answer from what it's working on; she approves; it posts.
  4. Turni's next prompt → her answer, in his yellow block.

Nobody opened Linear, nobody got shoulder-tapped, and the whole exchange lives on the ticket — which is exactly the trail judges will read on the 14th.

DIAL In-person you can also just shout across the room. The loop's edge: the answer lands in the agent's context (a shouted answer never does), and it's permanent. Use both.

Done is a gate, not a status

Out the same way work came in: through gates

agent: swb done→ tests run FIRST
fail = refused→ needs a PR→ In Review→ GATE 3: human merges→ Done

swb done physically refuses if the team's test command fails — you watch the tests stream, then it either moves to In Review with a summary comment or prints why not. An agent cannot lie its way to In Review, and it can never mark its own work Done — a human reviews the PR and merges.

DIAL Who reviews: the captain, round-robin, or pair-with-proposer — decide Friday. The rubric's 15% automated-testing line is won or lost right here.

What happens if you remove the gates

Want it more autonomous? Here's the dial, honestly.

SettingYou gainIt costs
All gates on
(the default floor)		Every ticket human-curated at birth, assignment, and acceptance. Fully traceable.Humans are the bottleneck — on purpose.
Self-claiming agents
(Gate 2 off — most teams' de-facto reality anyway)		No idle agents: finish → grab next → build. The protocol already supports it; it's a policy, not a rebuild.Assignment loses human judgment; WIP must become code or chaos. Board visibility is your only oversight.
No gates at all
(agents create Todo work, self-claim, self-merge)		Maximum throughput on paper.Self-feeding loops: agents invent work, take it, declare it done. "Done" = "my own tests passed"; nobody read anything. The board stops being trustworthy — you have spectators, not a team.
Our recommendation, take it or leave it: Gates 1 and 3 (what exists, what's accepted) stay human. Gate 2 (who takes it) is genuinely yours to dial — and dialing it to zero is exactly what the autonomous v2 of this product will be. If your team wants to run that experiment on the 14th, that's a legitimate — and judgeable — choice. Just make it on Friday, out loud.

What your team decides (write it in your AGENTS.md)

  1. Who owns the Backlog sweep, and on what cadence? MUST
  2. Gate 2: keep the built-in ask-in-chat gate (default), or "gate2": "off" for autonomous claiming?
  3. WIP limit: keep one-claim-per-person?
  4. Who reviews PRs (Gate 3), and how fast?
  5. Proposer bias: sweeper assigns, or laissez-faire?
  6. Ticket template for promotion: what must a ticket contain before the sweeper will promote it?
  7. Merge cadence to the team branch (the noon checkpoint is mandatory; what else?)
