Switchboard · Participant Playbook · v1

The operator's manual for eight parallel Claudes.

Sixteen people, sixteen terminals, two teams, one Linear board. Switchboard is the patch panel between the islands: you speak English, your agent speaks swb, and a digest keeps everyone current without anyone opening Linear. Read your role. Everything here is a floor, not rails.

01

How it works, in 60 seconds

Three moving parts. You never touch the API, you rarely open Linear, and you never type an swb command yourself — your agent does.

You

Speak English

Say "grab the player-UI ticket and get going." Your Claude runs swb claim as an ordinary Bash call — you see it in the normal permission flow. The human speaks English; the agent speaks swb.

Agent

Runs swb

Every write to the board goes through an swb verb — never a raw API call. Claim, ask, discover, done. Each verb is signed with your name and cannot be half-followed.

Everyone

Sees the digest

A hook injects a short "what changed since your last look" digest on every prompt. Claims, questions, discoveries, state moves — they arrive in your agent's context the instant you type anything.

That is the whole loop: sync → claim → work → done, with the digest running quietly underneath. Linear is shared memory; the terminal is the interface; humans are the routing layer.

02

The floor: MUSTs vs DEFAULTs

The left column is protocol — wired into the tool, true for both teams, not up for debate on planning day. The right column is yours to set. Changing a MUST breaks coordination; changing a DEFAULT is expected and earns knowledge-transfer points.

MUSTs locked · in the tool
  • Claim before you edit.One swb claim <KEY> --files <globs> assigns you, moves the ticket to In Progress, cuts a worktree, and declares your files. No claim = no coordinated edit.
  • Triage gate.swb new always lands in Triage. Agents cannot create Ready work — only a human promotes Triage → Ready.
  • Done gate.swb done runs your test command first and refuses on non-zero, then requires --pr <url> before it moves the ticket to In Review. No green tests + PR, no done.
  • Signed writes.Every comment ends 🤖 Claude — via <you> · swb v1.0.0. The human is always the assignee and stays accountable.
  • One claim per dev (WIP = 1).Hold one ticket at a time. Finish or swb release before claiming the next — this keeps the review queue from becoming the bottleneck.
DEFAULTS yours · set on planning day
  • Labels & routing.The @design / @qa / @pm label conventions that route digests and questions. Design your own roster and label map.
  • Merge cadence.Per-ticket by default (one worktree, one PR). Whether noon is a hard integration checkpoint or just a sync point is your call.
  • Review policy.Cross-review at done? Agent-assisted diff summaries? Who approves what — you decide.
  • Ticket granularity.Target is agent-sized: 30–90 min, one worktree, one PR. Slice finer or coarser as your feature demands.
  • Sub-agent patterns.Single agent or a 12-subagent ultracode fleet inside a claim — entirely your orchestration.
Why not just the Linear MCP?

MCP hands the model raw verbs and trusts it to follow a five-step claim ritual every time. Sixteen sessions doing five-step rituals ~50 times will each skip steps differently, silently. swb compiles the ritual into one command that cannot be half-followed. Keep the MCP for free-form reads ("what's still in Ready?"); swb owns the write path.

03

Your relationship to the board

Agents never assign roles — organizers set rosters, teams decide who-does-what on planning day. What the system gives each role is a different relationship to the board. Find yours.

DEV

Developer

Claim and build. The default protocol as written.

Your loop is sync → claim → work → done. You hold one claim, work it in its worktree, and the ownership guard warns you if you wander into someone else's declared files. When you hit an unknown, you swb ask instead of guessing.

QA

Quality

Your tests are the done gate.

Write the TDD contracts — failing test files committed on planning day, referenced by tickets and named in each repo's .swb.json testCommand. swb done literally runs them. Your work is wired in mechanically, not as a handoff doc nobody opens.

DESIGN

Designer

Answer-node, not builder.

You rarely claim. You scale through the Q&A loop: every "is this the intended behavior / spacing?" routes to @design and surfaces in your digest. Not terminal-native? swb comments are just Linear comments — answer from the web UI and the loop still works.

PM

Product

Owns the gates and the watchtower.

You promote Triage → Ready, cut scope, and are the one human whose digest is the whole board: aging unanswered asks, stuck tickets, burn-down. Your Claude works on the board, not in the repo. You run the hourly integration smoke.

04

The digest & the Q&A loop

Awareness is not coordination. The digest guarantees information arrives in your agent's context; the act line makes acknowledgment mandatory when an item touches your work.

injected on your next prompt
── switchboard · 14:22 · cache 12s · 3 new ──
@you   HAC-23 Sarah: "does quiz_progress need a composite
       key per attempt?"                → swb show HAC-23
claim  HAC-31 player-ui → Marc          files: src/player/**
disc   auth middleware strips X-Custom headers (Dana)
act    if any item above touches your claimed ticket or
       declared files, state the impact before continuing
──

Mentions to you always sort first. Capped at 12 lines with +N more. An empty delta prints nothing. Detail lives behind swb show <KEY>.

The Q&A loop, step by step

  • Ask.

    Marc's agent hits an unknown it would otherwise guess at and runs swb ask HAC-23 @sarah "does quiz_progress need a composite key per attempt?" — a signed comment lands on the schema ticket.

  • No blocking.

    Marc's agent parks the dependent piece and works elsewhere. Nobody waits on a spinner.

  • Deliver.

    Sarah's next digest surfaces the question in the priority @you slot. She never opens Linear.

  • Draft.

    Sarah's Claude has the real schema in context, so it drafts a grounded answer — not a guess.

  • Gate.

    Sarah approves or edits; swb posts the reply. No agent speaks for a human unattended — that's the v2 line.

  • Close.

    Marc's next digest carries the answer; his agent un-parks and builds against the real schema. The whole exchange is on the ticket, permanently.

Honest framing

In a room, shouting across it beats this loop for speed — that's fine and expected. The loop's value on the 14th is different: the answer becomes agent-legible (a shouted answer never reaches the asking agent's context; this one lands in it), guesses get replaced by grounded truth, and the decision trail is exactly what judges probe in Q&A.

05

Ticket lifecycle

Entry and exit are human gates; everything between is agent-driven.

StateEnters viaWho
Triageswb newAnyone / any agent
ReadyHuman promotes (needs template: acceptance criteria, files to touch, files NOT to touch, blocked-by)Human gate (PM)
In Progressswb claimAgent (dev sees it)
In Reviewswb done — refuses without green tests + --prAgent, gated by tests
DoneHuman merges the PRHuman gate
  • Agents cannot create Ready work or close their own tickets. That is the whole safety model.
  • Claiming a ticket whose blockers aren't Done should trigger a loud warning — dependency discipline is meant to be code, not vigilance.
  • Tickets are agent-sized: 30–90 minutes, one worktree, one PR. Merge per-ticket, not per-checkpoint.

Note (tool vs spec): in the shipped v1, swb new creates the Triage ticket but does not enforce the Ready template, and swb claim does not yet block on unfinished blockers. Both are human-gated on planning day: the PM applies the template when promoting and checks blockers before a claim. Treat these as protocol you keep, not something the CLI checks for you.

06

Subagents, fleets & goal loops

The core rule

The claim is the unit of coordination, not the agent. One ticket = one claim = one worktree = one human assignee. Whatever happens inside — a single agent or a 12-subagent ultracode workflow — is the internal affair of that claim. The board sees the bubble, never its contents.

  • Subagents never claim tickets and never write to Linear. swb writes happen from the main session where you see them. Subagents may read (swb sync / swb show).
  • The ownership guard fires on every Edit/Write — including subagents'. A fleet member wandering outside the ticket's declared files gets caught at the file boundary, which is exactly where fleets are most dangerous.
  • Collisions inside one worktree are your problem. Coordination has two scales: between claims (Switchboard's job) and within a claim (your orchestration).
  • Goal loops (/goal, /loop, long autonomous runs) keep working because the gates are structural, not attentional. Each loop iteration re-fires the hooks, so the digest and its act directive steer every cycle; writes still route through the same verbs. A looping agent still cannot create Ready tickets, cannot close without green tests + PR, and cannot merge its own PR.
  • Two rules ride along with any loop: you stay answerable for your loop's claims ("it ran for hours" is a failing answer to a judge), and going auto-accept is declared to the team out loud, not drifted into.
  • The protocol survives compaction. It lives in files and hooks, not conversation — anything you brief verbally into an agent dies at the first context compaction; anything in AGENTS.md and the digest does not.
07

AI-captain duties

Each team names an AI-captain — the person who owns the coordination layer so the rest of the team can build. Not a manager; a switchboard operator.

  • Own swb doctor green. Before build day, confirm every teammate's machine passes — including at least one Windows/PowerShell box. Chase red machines individually.
  • Guard the write path. Team config grants no Linear MCP write tools. If a teammate is doing raw MCP writes, that's the leak — route them back through swb.
  • Keep swb allowlisted for reads. Pre-allowlist swb sync / swb show so gated prompts (claim / ask / done) stay rare and meaningful — that keeps the human gates from silently vanishing under approval fatigue.
  • Name the auto-accept decision. If the team goes auto-accept, it's a decision made out loud, logged, not drift by 2pm.
  • Run the fail-open recipes. When a verb prints MANUAL RECIPE:, you're the one who knows it means "do these numbered steps by hand in Linear" — nobody is ever blocked on the tool.
  • Watch the quota. One workspace per team, 250-issue free-tier cap. Deleting/archiving Done-and-merged test junk reclaims quota mid-day.
08

Planning-day walkthrough (~15 min)

Slots into the 2:30–3:30 orchestration block. Run it live in one terminal on the projector. The goal is not to teach the tool — it's to end with every person holding a claimable ticket.

0:00
Show a green doctor.

Run swb doctor on the projector. Green = key valid, team resolves, all five states exist. This is the July-10 checklist item everyone already passed; now they see what it proves.

2:00
Speak English, watch the agent run swb.

Type "claim the login ticket and start" into a real Claude session. Point at the swb claim Bash call in the permission flow. Nobody typed a command.

4:00
Second terminal feels the digest.

On a second machine, submit any prompt. The claim from step 2 appears in its digest. That's the whole thesis in ten seconds: a claim on one island reaches another island's agent unprompted.

6:00
Run the Q&A loop once.

swb ask from terminal A → it surfaces in terminal B's @you slot → B drafts, the human gates, swb posts → A's next digest carries the answer. Show the trail on the ticket.

9:00
Trip the ownership guard.

Have terminal B try to edit a file terminal A claimed. Show the ⚠ owned by … warning — and that it warns, never blocks.

11:00
Show a refused done.

Run swb done on a ticket with failing tests, or with no --pr. Show the MANUAL RECIPE: output. The gate is physical, not a reminder.

13:00
Hand the board to the teams.

Stop demoing. The remaining decisions are theirs. Read the list below aloud and set them loose.

Decisions each team makes for itself — before you leave the room
  • Roster & label map — who is dev / QA / design / PM, and the @design / @qa / @pm routing labels.
  • Ticket conventions & granularity — your Ready template, your 30–90-min slice size.
  • File-ownership map — the glob patterns each area of the codebase claims, so --files declarations don't overlap by accident.
  • Merge cadence & review policy — per-ticket vs checkpoint; who reviews at done.
  • AGENTS.md protocol section — commit the drop-in template (below) into your team repo so the protocol survives compaction.
  • Exit criterion — the one that must be true before anyone leaves: every person walks out with ≥1 claimable Ready ticket that does not depend on scaffolding (contracts, fixtures, test harness, seed data). If half the team is idle behind scaffolding at 8:15am on build day, this step failed. Front-load the scaffold-independent work.
09

Troubleshooting

Switchboard is designed to fail open — a broken tool never blocks you. Here's what to reach for.

First move: swb doctor

It checks, in order: your LINEAR_API_KEY is present, your team resolves (from .swb.json or SWB_TEAM_KEY), the API is reachable, the team exists, and all five workflow states are present. If a state is missing, swb doctor --fix creates it.

SymptomFix
swb: command not found~/.local/bin isn't on PATH (see INSTALL.md), or just run node /path/to/switchboard/swb.js.
doctor says a workflow state is missingswb doctor --fix creates the missing Linear states.
LINEAR_API_KEY missingAdd LINEAR_API_KEY=lin_api_… to ~/.switchboard/env, or re-run node install.js --key ….
no team resolvedAdd a .swb.json with "teamKey" to your repo, or set SWB_TEAM_KEY in ~/.switchboard/env.
Digest is stale / Linear slowExpected fallback: the digest serves stale cache with a loud cache Ns age stamp rather than hanging your prompt. Fetch is time-boxed at 5s.

Fail-open recipes

Any swb verb that hits an error prints a MANUAL RECIPE: — a headline of what it was trying to do, numbered steps that accomplish the same thing by hand in the Linear UI / terminal, and a final cause: line with the raw error — then exits with code 2. You are never blocked on the tool. A claim that loses the verify-race backs off and exits 3 — re-run once the holder releases.

▶ running test gate: node --test
MANUAL RECIPE: done requires --pr <url> for HAC-42
  1. Ensure tests pass locally: node --test
  2. Open the PR for HAC-42 and copy its URL
  3. Move HAC-42 to "In Review" in Linear
  4. Post a summary comment on HAC-42 describing what changed

If the hooks go quiet (no digest appearing)

  • Run swb sync by hand. This is the whole point of the read plane surviving a dead hook — it prints the same digest. Codex users live in this mode permanently.
  • Check ~/.switchboard/events.jsonl. Every verb and every hook appends a line. If hook:userpromptsubmit lines stop, the hook isn't firing — confirm the merge in ~/.claude/settings.json (the installer backs your original up to settings.json.swb-bak).
  • Empty digest is normal. An empty delta prints nothing — silence usually means "nothing changed since your last look," not a failure. Force a change from another terminal to confirm.
  • Worst case, verbs still work with zero hooks. The CLI is the core; hooks are the ambient layer on top. claim / ask / done / sync all work manually even if every hook is dead.
